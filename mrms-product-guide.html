Understanding the MRMS Radar Products

A quick guide to the four radar layers on the nowcast page. MRMS (Multi-Radar Multi-Sensor) is a national NWS system that blends data from every nearby radar into a single high-resolution picture, updated every couple of minutes.

Base Reflectivity

What's happening now

This is the "regular" radar view — the same kind of colored radar image you see on TV and weather apps. It shows where rain (or hail/snow) is falling and how intense the echoes are at that moment.

Read it as: "Where is it raining right now, and how hard?" Brighter colors mean stronger returns. It is a snapshot of intensity, not a measure of how much water has actually accumulated.

Radar-Only QPE — 15 Minute

Short-burst rainfall

QPE stands for Quantitative Precipitation Estimate — radar's best estimate of how much rain has actually fallen. This layer totals the rainfall over the most recent 15 minutes.

Read it as: "How much rain just came down in the last 15 minutes?" This is the layer to watch for sudden, intense cloudbursts that drive flash flooding in canyons and on burn scars.

Values shown in inches of accumulated rainfall.

Radar-Only QPE — 1 Hour

Recent rainfall total

The same radar rainfall estimate as above, but accumulated over the past full hour. It smooths out short bursts and shows the bigger picture of where meaningful rain has piled up.

Read it as: "How much rain has fallen over the last hour?" Useful for judging whether a storm has been sitting over one area long enough to be a concern.

Values shown in inches of accumulated rainfall.

QPE-to-FFG Ratio — 1 Hour

Flash flood risk signal

This compares the past hour of rainfall (QPE) against Flash Flood Guidance (FFG) — the amount of rain the NWS estimates an area can absorb before flooding begins. It is expressed as a percentage.

Read it as: "Has it rained enough to cause flooding?" At 100% or more, rainfall has met or exceeded what the ground can handle, and flash flooding becomes likely. This is the most direct flood-risk layer of the four.

Values shown as a percentage of flash flood guidance.

Important — Please Read

These radar products are decision-support tools, not official forecasts or warnings. Radar estimates rainfall remotely and can over- or under-estimate actual amounts, especially in mountainous terrain, during heavy hail, or far from the nearest radar.

QPE and FFG layers are estimates and should always be used alongside official National Weather Service watches, warnings, and statements, as well as ground reports and observed conditions. Nothing on this page replaces an official NWS product.

This page is provided for situational awareness only. Make all life-safety and operational decisions in coordination with the National Weather Service and your local emergency management protocols.

Data source: NOAA/NWS Multi-Radar Multi-Sensor (MRMS) system.
