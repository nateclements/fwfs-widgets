The representative storm

One storm shape, scaled five ways.
Every scenario on the map is built from a single representative storm: a typical monsoon cloudburst derived from about ten real storms over these watersheds. The percentages do not change the storm's timing or shape. They scale only how much total rain falls.

What the percentages mean

The representative storm drops roughly an inch and a half of rain over about two hours, with most of it falling in the first 30 to 45 minutes, the way a real monsoon burst does. The 50% to 150% scenarios take that exact same storm and turn its total rainfall up or down: 50% is half as much rain, 150% is half again as much. The rain still arrives on the same schedule in every scenario, just wetter or drier overall.

Why this matters for reading the map: when the system shows you a 125% scenario, it is not predicting a different kind of storm. It is telling you that live radar is measuring rainfall close to one and a quarter times the representative storm, so the pre-modeled flooding for that rainfall level is the closest available match.
When rainfall exceeds the largest modeled scenario, the map holds at 150% rather than extrapolating, since no flood model was run above that level. If you see the 150% scenario during an unusually intense storm, treat it as a floor: actual flooding may be more extensive, deeper, or arrive faster than what is shown.
