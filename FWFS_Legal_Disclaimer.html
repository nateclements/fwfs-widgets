Grand County Flood Warning and Forecast System (FWFS)

Developed and delivered by Consor North America, Inc. Operated in partnership with the Grand County Office of Emergency Management, Moab, Utah.

Purpose and Scope

The Grand County Flood Warning and Forecast System (FWFS) is a decision-support tool developed by Consor North America, Inc. to assist trained emergency management professionals in monitoring and assessing flood threat conditions across the Spanish Valley and greater Moab area. This application aggregates publicly available meteorological forecast data, radar products, and automated scoring algorithms to provide situational awareness support. It does not constitute an official flood forecast, flash flood warning, or emergency directive of any kind, nor is this application a substitute for professional judgment, training, advice, or analysis.

Risk Scores Are Guidance, Not Guarantees

The automated scoring framework within the FWFS integrates National Weather Service forecast products, radar-estimated precipitation, and flash flood guidance data to produce composite threat scores across four pre-event time windows. These scores are guidance benchmarks based on generalized hydrometeorological thresholds. They do not account for every site-specific condition — including antecedent soil moisture, channel capacity, debris loading, terrain variability, and storm-scale behavior — that can influence flash flood onset and severity in canyon-country environments. Flooding may occur at score levels below action thresholds, and high scores do not guarantee that flooding will occur.

Flash Flood Guidance (FFG) Is a Generalized Estimate

FFG values used within this application are basin-scale estimates produced by the NWS Colorado Basin River Forecast Center. They are statistical approximations of the rainfall amount needed to produce bankfull or greater streamflow and are not real-time, site-specific measurements. FFG exceedance should not be treated as a definitive indicator that flooding is occurring or imminent; FFG non-exceedance should not be interpreted as a guarantee that flooding will not occur.

Radar and Remotely Sensed Data Have Inherent Limitations

Radar and multi-sensor precipitation products displayed within the FWFS — including MRMS QPE, Base Reflectivity, and QPE-to-FFG Ratio — are subject to error from beam blockage, range degradation, bright-band contamination, hail contamination, and complex terrain effects common to canyon-country environments. Radar-only QPE is an estimate of rainfall, not a direct gauge measurement, and may underestimate or overestimate accumulation in any given storm event.

Users must not interpret an absence of displayed data as an absence of precipitation or flood threat. Human Judgment Is Essential and Irreplaceable

The FWFS is one component of a broader operational decision-support framework. Emergency managers are expected to use this application in conjunction with real-time NWS watches, warnings, and advisories; direct field observation; stream gauge and rain gauge network data; coordination with NWS Grand Junction forecasters; and their professional knowledge of local terrain, drainage behavior, and community conditions. No automated system can fully account for the complexity of real-world flash flood events. The final authority for all protective actions, public notifications, and emergency response decisions rests solely with the responsible emergency management official.
